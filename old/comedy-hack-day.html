The Los Angeles Comedy Hackathon

On December 5th-7th, Los Angeles played host to an event called "Comedy Hack Day," and to the benefit the event, it turns out that LA is an easy place to find people who are interested in building technology, making others laugh, and building technology that makes others laugh.

Comedy Hack Day is a themed hackathon put on by digital agency Cultivated Wit, which is itself composed of former staffers from satirical news rag The Onion.

According to Cultivated Wit, the first Comedy Hack Day began a few years back as an attempt to unite their awkward comedian friends with their awkward software developer friends. Fittingly, to this day the hackathon still attracts its fair share of nerdy and funny people with questionable social skills; however, with a growing number of participants, it has also gotten the attention of a handful of sponsors, including- to the joy of Los Angeles' attendees- California's own Lagunitas Brewing Company.

Besides the free beer, what makes Comedy Hack Day different from a typical hackathon is that its participants work on humor-oriented projects. As a good example of one of these projects, see the app Me, The Internet, which "makes the news interesting again by injecting your name and picture into every news article." So, for instance, while reading the Times, a user of Me, The Internet might see a headline about her most recent speech to the American people, alongside a smiling photo of herself.

This past December was the first time that the hack day had been hosted in LA, which made for an especially ridiculous tour de farce. It seems Los Angeles is a perfect place to round up funny entertainers and brilliant technologists, perhaps because it is both a popular destination for aspiring comedians and home to the tech-friendly Silicon Beach.

But beyond turning out a wealth of eager participants, Los Angeles also provided the event with some suitably eccentric quarters - namely, at the former location of Howard Hughes' private airport, which is now YouTube's LA production space.

Thus, the hackathon took place adjacent to a helicopter-monument erected in memory of Howard Hughes, and as the first night wore on, the studio teemed with humorous hackers hunched over MacBooks, seemingly oblivious to the bustling of well-established YouTube creators in the background.

Once the clacking of keyboards subsided, each group of participants gave a public demo of their product in front of a live audience and a panel of celebrity judges, the latter of whom had the final say on which project received the event's coveted grand prize.

The judges at Comedy Hack Day are instructed to look for teams with both strong tech and an entertaining presentation, and in that regard, the winners of past Comedy Hack Days served as exemplary models for them. For instance, earlier in 2014, at a Comedy Hack Day in San Francisco, the grand prize went to a wearable, voice-activated Furby.

The winner of Comedy Hack Day LA was an app that certainly lived up to the standard of having a side-splitting presentation. Dubbed ShtTalk, it was a messaging application for people who tend to go on their phone whilst in the restroom. The app was location-based, so a user could chat with others in nearby lavatories.

For their demo, the creators snuck one of their teammates onto the stage with a fake ceramic toilet bowl before the presentations started, placing him underneath a large sheet.
